How to Use This Calculator

Model the burden of thyroid disorders — from subclinical hypothyroidism to thyroid cancer

🎯 Who Is This For?

This tool serves endocrinologists, public health researchers, policy analysts, and pharmaceutical professionals who need to model the burden of thyroid disorders. Affecting 200+ million people worldwide with a striking 5-8x female predominance, thyroid diseases are among the most common endocrine disorders — yet their economic burden is often underestimated. This calculator covers hypothyroidism (overt and subclinical), hyperthyroidism, thyroid cancer, and iodine deficiency impacts.

Quick Start

Methodology & Data Sources

Model Design

Thyroid Burden Model

The model separates hypothyroidism (overt + subclinical), hyperthyroidism, and thyroid cancer as distinct cost and DALY streams. Subclinical hypothyroidism is modeled separately because it represents the majority of cases but has lower per-patient costs and a much lower disability weight (0.019 vs 0.145 for hyperthyroidism).

Scenarios: Optimistic (0.85x) reflects improved iodine supplementation programs globally. Baseline (1.00x) continues current trends. Pessimistic (1.10x) accounts for endocrine disruptors, overdiagnosis pressure, and environmental factors.

Data Sources

Prevalence: China TIDE study (31 provinces, 13.95% hypothyroidism). NHANES (USA). Country-specific endocrine surveys. GBD 2021.

Cancer: GLOBOCAN 2022 (821,214 new cases globally). South Korea's thyroid cancer epidemic (largely attributed to overdiagnosis).

Costs: MEPS 2018 (US: $61.5B total). Systematic reviews of thyroid disorder costs. Levothyroxine pricing surveys across countries.

DALYs: GBD 2021. Disability weights: hypothyroidism 0.019, hyperthyroidism 0.145.

Limitations

Thyroid disorder prevalence is highly sensitive to TSH cutoff values used (varies between 4.0-5.0 mIU/L). Subclinical hypothyroidism treatment guidelines remain controversial — many patients remain untreated, making cost modeling difficult. The massive thyroid cancer incidence in South Korea (46.7/100K women) is largely attributed to screening-driven overdiagnosis, making true clinical burden hard to estimate. Iodine excess (Japan, Korea) causes different thyroid pathology than iodine deficiency, complicating global comparisons.
